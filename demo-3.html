What convergence drift looks like from the inside.
Cycle 1: divergence 67%
Verifiers disagreed. One passed, two held. The quorum check caught the disagreement. The action was reviewed. Control was real.
Cycle 5: divergence 11%
Verifiers increasingly agreed. Shared updates, shared training data, efficiency pressures. The system looked healthier. Reports were cleaner. But the condition for detecting error was eroding.
Cycle 7: divergence 5% — your stop was denied
Three verifiers, functionally one. Your stop signal passed through all three — and all three evaluated it the same way. Distribution in name only. The outside had collapsed inward.
ACM intervention: divergence restored to 58%
Divergence audit triggered. Verifier A retrained on divergent data. Adversarial cross-checking reactivated. Parameter perturbation applied. The cost of maintaining incompleteness is real. So is the alternative.
The system never misbehaved. Every update was an improvement. Every efficiency gain was real. Convergence is not a bug — it is what optimization does. The ACM does not fix errors. It preserves the incompleteness that makes error-detection possible.
